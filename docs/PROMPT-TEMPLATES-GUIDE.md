## Beginner Learning Path: Zero AI Experience → Real Value in One Afternoon (or One Week)

This path was designed for someone who has **never** successfully used an AI before, or who is too tired/scared to try. It assumes you may be on a shared device, have a child or family member nearby, and feel like "this isn't for people like me."

**Total time if you spread it out:** 60–90 minutes across several days.  
**Minimum to feel real power today:** 12 minutes on the Grounding prompt template.

### Phase 1 — Permission & Safety (10–15 minutes, today if possible)

**Prompt template to use:** Grounding when you feel like shit (5-minute version)

- Open Banal (or any free chatbot).
- Choose the grounding prompt template.
- Fill only these two things honestly: your name (or "friend") and one or two words for how you feel right now ("overwhelmed", "numb", "angry and tired", "like I can't do one more thing").
- For "what you have right now" write something true: "bed", "library chair", "phone with 11% battery", "nothing".
- Send.
- Read the result slowly. You do not have to do the tiny win if it feels like too much.

**Self-check after:**

- Did any sentence make you feel like a real person instead of a problem to be fixed?
- Did the ending line give you even 5 seconds of not hating yourself?
- If the answer is "a little" or "yes" — you just used world-class AI support for free. You succeeded.

If it helped at all, save the output (screenshot or copy into a note). Come back to this prompt template whenever the static in your head gets too loud.

### Phase 2 — Decode the Scary Paper (15–25 minutes, when you have a form or letter)

**Prompt template:** Decode any scary form or letter

Real trigger example: A letter from the welfare office, a hospital bill with numbers that make no sense, a landlord notice, a benefits denial.

- Paste or describe the scary part (you can paraphrase if typing the whole thing is too much).
- Fill the blanks with whatever you know: "welfare denial letter", the deadline if there is one, what you are most afraid it means.
- Send.

**What you will get:** Plain words + the 1–3 things that actually matter + exact next steps (phone numbers, what to say, what to bring) + smart questions that protect your rights.

**Self-check:**

- Do you understand one more thing than you did before?
- Is there at least one concrete action you can take in the next 48 hours that feels slightly less impossible?
- Did it say "this is not legal advice" while still giving you real options? (That is correct and respectful.)

If yes, you just turned a panic document into something you can act on. That is the kind of real power the rich pay for.

### Phase 3 — Turn Your Real Life Into Something That Gets You the Job or the Benefit (20–30 minutes)

**Prompt templates to use (pick one):**

- Job applications: turn gaps into strengths
- STAR stories from caregiving & real life
- Bureaucracy & rights: calm, effective letters

Example real day: You need a cover letter for a job that "requires" experience you don't have on paper because you were the one keeping your family alive. Or you need to write the appeal to the housing office because the denial letter came yesterday.

- Use the form in Banal. Be brutally honest in the "gapSituation" or "caregivingSituation" field — one sentence, no apology.

- Copy the output. Read it out loud once.

**Self-check:**

- Does the letter or story name the hard thing without sounding like an excuse?
- Does it end with dignity instead of begging?
- Could a tired, real human being have written this?

If it feels "too good," reply to the AI: "Make the first paragraph sound more like how a tired single parent would actually talk — shorter sentences, less polished."

You just did in 15 minutes what used to require a $200/hr career coach or advocate.

### 1. Job applications: turn gaps into strengths

**就職活動：空白期間を強みに変える**

**The Heart of This Prompt Template**

Life created gaps. Caregiving for a dying parent. Your own health crash. Unemployment that lasted longer than planned. Moving across the country with nothing. Raising children while the world assumed you had nothing else to do.

The rich pay career coaches $300 an hour to turn those exact periods into "evidence of resilience and project management under pressure." You deserve the same translation — without paying, without apology, and without the corporate voice that makes you sound like you are lying.

This prompt template takes the honest, sometimes ugly truth of what you lived through and turns it into language that hiring managers are trained to recognize as strength. It never uses the words "even though" or "despite." It never asks you to be grateful for your pain. It simply says: this is what I did. These are the muscles I built. Here is why that matters for the job you are trying to get.

It is written for the person who has 12 minutes on a library computer, whose hands are shaking, and who is terrified that the blank space on the resume will end the conversation before it starts.

**When to Reach for It**

- The job posting says "2+ years experience" and your paid work history has a 14-month hole.
- You are a returning parent, a caregiver, someone who was sick, someone who was the one keeping everyone else alive.
- You need both a cover letter paragraph and resume bullets that do not make you sound broken.
- You want the letter to sound like a real adult wrote it — warm, direct, human.

**3–5 Concrete Real-Life Examples**

**Example 1 — The 15-minute library sprint with a crying toddler**  
Situation: You are at the public library. Your 2-year-old is in the stroller and starting to cry because they are hungry and tired. You have exactly 15 minutes before you have to leave to catch the only bus that gets you home before dark. You are applying for a part-time stocking job at a big box store. The posting asks for "reliable attendance and 1 year customer service." Your last paid job was 18 months ago at a small shop that closed; before that you spent 13 months full-time caring for your father who had dementia while your sibling worked two jobs.

What you type into the blanks (you can do this in 4 minutes):

- yourName: Alex Rivera
- targetRole: Part-time stocker / overnight team member
- gapSituation: Full-time cared for my father with advanced dementia for 13 months while also working part-time until the shop closed; the 18-month gap on paper is because I was the only one who could keep him safe and at home.
- yourStrengths: Managed complex medication schedules and daily care under constant stress with zero outside help; kept a household running on under $50/week by meal planning, couponing, and trading favors with neighbors; learned to stay calm and solve problems when everything was falling apart.
- tone: warm and direct

What the prompt template gives you (ready to copy):
A short cover letter paragraph that names the caregiving once, immediately connects it to "I know how to show up when the team is short and the day is long," and ends with the dignity line.

Resume bullets under "Additional Experience" that sound like: "Coordinated 24/7 care for family member with dementia while maintaining part-time employment — developed systems for tracking medications, appointments, and supplies under extreme time pressure."

**Self-check:**

- Does the gap get mentioned only once and then immediately turned into proof you are reliable?
- Does it feel like something you could actually say out loud to a person?
- Did reading it make the 18-month hole feel smaller instead of bigger?

**Example 2 — The night before the deadline, on a cracked phone**  
You are in a shelter intake office or at home with the lights low because you can't afford the bill this month. The application for a city job training program is due at 9 a.m. You have a 9-month gap because you were in a domestic violence situation and had to leave everything. You have never written a cover letter in your life.

The prompt template will help you write a version that says the truth ("I left an unsafe situation and rebuilt from zero") without trauma-dumping, and connects it directly to "I know how to protect my own safety and the safety of a workplace."

**Example 3 — Returning to work after your own long illness**  
You spent 11 months in and out of treatment for something that almost killed you. Your friends and family call you a warrior. You feel like a ghost. The job is an entry-level admin role at a clinic. The prompt template turns "I survived my own body trying to end me" into "I bring deep patience with people who are scared and in pain, because I have been both."

**Example 4 — The parent whose "gap" was actually three kids under five**  
No paid work for four years. You were the one who kept three small humans alive, fed, clean, and on time for every appointment while your partner worked 60-hour weeks. The prompt template translates "I was a stay-at-home parent" into concrete evidence of logistics, crisis management, teaching, and endurance that most 22-year-olds with a degree do not have.

**Example 5 — The person who moved across the country with nothing after a breakup or family death**  
Sixteen-month gap. You drove 2,000 miles with $180 and a duffel bag. You slept in the car for three weeks while looking for work. The prompt template helps you write the story as "I can start over from zero, stay calm under pressure, and show up every day even when the world has taken everything else."

**Self-Check Questions (Do These After You Try It)**

- Did the output use the word "even though" or "despite"? (If yes, the AI did not follow the rules — ask it to remove those phrases.)
- Does the cover letter or bullets make you sound like a capable adult who lived a real life, not a victim or a superhero?
- Is there at least one concrete number or verb from your actual days in the text?
- After reading it, do you feel even 10% more willing to hit "submit"?
- Does the very last line of the prompt template response give you a small piece of your dignity back?

If you can answer "yes" or "a little" to three of these, it worked.

**Best Practices for Using With Any Free AI (Copy-Paste Ready)**

Inside Banal: just pick the prompt template and fill the form. It is already perfect.

Outside Banal (ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, etc.):

1. Copy the exact English or Japanese template from Banal's Prompt Templates panel (it is the source of truth).
2. Replace the {{variables}} with your real sentences. Be short and honest. One sentence for the gap is enough.
3. Paste the whole thing.
4. After the first output, you can follow up with:
   - "Make the first paragraph 20% shorter and sound more like a tired person who still shows up."
   - "Add one specific example from my strengths that I forgot to mention: [one sentence]."
   - "Rewrite the resume bullets so they use only verbs I actually did — no corporate words like 'leveraged' or 'synergized'."

Japanese users: When applying to Japanese companies or writing to Japanese government programs, always use the Japanese template version. It uses the right level of keigo without sounding stiff or desperate.

**Critical rule:** Never let the AI add shame language. If it does, reply: "Remove every phrase that sounds like an apology or an excuse. Keep only the facts and the strengths."

**Dignity line that must appear (or you add it):** "Your life did not make you less qualified. It made you the person they need."

### 2. Learn anything on zero budget

**予算ゼロで何でも学ぶ**

**The Heart of This Prompt Template**

You want to learn something that could change your options — how to pass a certification, how to speak better English for a job, how to fix small engines, how to help your kid with math, how to understand your own medical condition, how to write better letters.

The rich pay for tutors, courses, and apps that cost more than your monthly food budget.

This prompt template builds you a 7-day plan that uses only what already exists for free: the library, YouTube (with captions), Wikipedia, public domain books, teaching what you just learned to a neighbor or your child, free Anki on the web, the notebook you already have.

It is built for the person who has 15–25 minutes while the baby naps or on the bus or after everyone else is asleep. It assumes you are tired. It gives you permission to skip a day without guilt. It measures progress in "I taught one sentence to my kid" instead of "I finished chapter 7."

**When to Reach for It**

- You have a test or interview in two weeks and zero money for prep.
- You want to understand a form or a diagnosis or a contract so you stop feeling stupid.
- Your child is struggling in school and you want to help but you were never good at that subject.
- You are trying to get back into the workforce and one skill is the missing piece.
- You just want to feel your brain working again on something that is not survival.

**3–5 Concrete Real-Life Examples**

**Example 1 — The parent who needs to help with 4th grade math on a phone with 8% battery**  
You never finished high school. Your 9-year-old is crying over fractions. The teacher sent home a worksheet. You have 18 minutes before you have to start the evening routine.

The plan the prompt template gives: Day 1 — 12 minutes on the library computer (or phone): search "Khan Academy fractions 4th grade" (free, no account), watch the first 4-minute video with your child sitting next to you. Then spend 4 minutes teaching your child the one thing you just learned, using the worksheet as the example. Write on the back of the worksheet in pen: "Today we learned the top number is numerator."

End of plan includes: "If you only do this one day and nothing else, you still showed your child that learning is something adults do with them, not something that happens at school while they feel dumb."

**Example 2 — The person on the night shift who wants to learn enough English to pass a job interview in three weeks**  
You work 11pm–7am. You have 20 minutes on the bus home and 15 minutes before you have to sleep. You can use the free library Wi-Fi for 30 minutes on your day off.

The 7-day plan is built exactly around those tiny windows: 8 minutes of YouTube "English job interview questions for [your industry]" with captions on, 7 minutes repeating the answers out loud in the stairwell, 5 minutes teaching the phrase "I am reliable and I show up on time" to your roommate or your reflection in the mirror.

**Example 3 — Understanding your own or your parent's medical diagnosis with no medical background**  
You just got a letter with words like "comorbidity" and "prognosis" and a bunch of numbers. You are scared and ashamed that you don't understand what it means for your life.

The plan: Use Wikipedia + the library's free medical encyclopedia access (many libraries have it). One 15-minute session: type the scariest word into Wikipedia, read only the first two paragraphs, write in your own words on paper what you think it means. Then one "teach it back" to a friend or even to the plant in the window: "This word means the illness has more than one thing going on at the same time."

Next day: go back and add one more sentence.

**Example 4 — Learning to write better letters to the welfare office or your child's school**  
You keep getting replies that say "insufficient information." You want to learn how to make your letters clearer without sounding like a lawyer.

The plan uses free public domain books on clear writing (many on Project Gutenberg or library apps), plus the bureaucracy-letters prompt template itself as practice material. You write one tiny practice letter every other day and "teach" the structure to your notebook.

**Example 5 — The person who wants to learn a small repair skill to save money (changing a bike tire, basic sewing, fixing a leaky faucet)**  
You have a bike that is your only transportation. The shop wants $40 to fix a flat. You have $6.

The plan: Library computer → search "how to change a bike tire flat with only a screwdriver and pump" on YouTube (there are 3-minute videos with no talking, just hands). Watch once. Then go outside with the bike and the phone. Pause the video every 30 seconds and do the step. If you mess up, that's the lesson. Teach your neighbor kid the one thing you got right.

**Self-Check Questions**

- Did the plan ask you for more than 30 minutes on any single day?
- Is there a "teach it back" step every day (this is the part that actually makes it stick when you are tired)?
- Does it give you explicit permission to skip a day and still be proud of yourself?
- Can you do the first day's activity with the device and time you actually have right now?
- After reading the plan, do you feel "maybe I could do the first 12 minutes" instead of "this is impossible"?

**Best Practices for Any Free AI**

- Always tell the AI your real available time and your real obstacles ("I only have the bus ride and my phone has no headphones and my kid is with me").
- Ask for specific free resource names: "Name three actual free YouTube channels with captions for absolute beginners in [topic], not popular ones that assume you already know things."
- After the plan, reply: "Give me the exact first 12-minute session written as a checklist I can follow while tired and distracted."
- Track progress on paper or in your phone notes with one line per day: "Watched 4 min video. Taught my kid the word 'numerator'. Felt stupid but did it anyway."

For Japanese: The Japanese template will suggest Japanese free resources (NHK for School videos, library apps that are actually free in Japan, public domain books on Aozora Bunko, etc.).

**Dignity line:** "You are not behind. 15 minutes a day with a broke person who keeps showing up beats 3 hours a week that never happens."

You go to their house for 90 minutes, they provide the boxes and trash bags, you leave them with systems that actually work. $40–60 cash for the session. First customer: the tired mom two doors down who already complained about the mess last week.

Target: small businesses owned by older immigrants who hate forms, or people who need to write to their kid's school. First step: put a handwritten note in the language of the community on the laundromat bulletin board.

Errand running for people who just had surgery or who have newborns: pick up prescriptions, groceries, one load of laundry from the laundromat. $8–12 per trip. Safety rules built in (never go inside someone's house the first time, only take cash, tell one person where you are going).

### 4. Bureaucracy & rights: calm, effective letters

**役所・権利：冷静で効く手紙**

**The Heart of This Prompt Template**

You need to write to the welfare office, the housing authority, the unemployment office, your landlord, the hospital billing department, or a government agency. The letter must be read by an overwhelmed human who sees 200 letters a day. It must state facts, claim rights, make one clear request, and not make you sound angry or pathetic.

The system is built to make you feel small and powerless. Those in power want you quiet, ashamed, and giving up. This prompt template is your weapon against that.

This prompt template writes the letter for you in the correct formal structure for your country. In English it is plain, respectful, and firm. In Japanese it uses proper keigo (です・ます, いただきたく存じます, いただけますと幸いです) throughout — never overly humble, never corporate, always the voice of a calm adult who knows they are allowed to ask for what they need.

We write the letters that make the powerful listen.

It ends with the instruction to print two copies and send one by certified mail or with read receipt if possible.

**When to Reach for It**

- You received a denial or a scary notice and the deadline is soon.
- You have tried calling and got nowhere or were treated badly.
- You need a paper trail.
- You want the letter to sound like it was written by someone who has their life together, even on the days you feel like you are falling apart.

**3–5 Concrete Real-Life Examples**

**Example 1 — Welfare / benefits denial appeal (you have 10 days to respond)**

### 8. Debt & hardship scripts that actually work

It starts with the most important sentence: "Important: You are not a bad person for needing this conversation. Many people in your exact situation have successfully used versions of this script."

**When to Reach for It**

- A bill collector is calling or you know one will soon.
- You got a "final notice" from utilities or landlord.
- You want to negotiate a medical bill or credit card before it goes to collections.
- You are about to miss a payment and you want to be proactive with dignity.

**3–5 Concrete Real-Life Examples**

**Example 1 — $2,400 credit card debt, you can realistically pay $45/month right now, hardship is medical + job loss**  
Phone script opening: "Hello, my name is [Name], account number [number]. I am calling because I want to make arrangements to pay what I can actually sustain so I do not default again."

The script is slightly different because it is a person who can evict you. More emphasis on "I want to stay in this home and pay you what I can."

**Example 4 — Utility shutoff notice**  
The script asks for a payment plan or extension and also for the utility's low-income or hardship program (many have them and do not advertise loudly).

**Example 5 — Collection agency that bought your old debt for pennies on the dollar**  
You can often settle for 30–50% if you have a lump sum or a very strict plan. The script knows how to say this without giving away too much.

**Self-Check Questions**

- Is the opening calm and adult?
- Does the ask include the specific dollar amount and start date?
- Is there a clear "I need it in writing" step?
- After reading the script, do you feel like a capable person who is handling their business instead of a criminal?
